The Autonomy Test: Why Healthy Relationships Prioritize Individual Interests Over Constant Closeness

When I first started dating my partner, we operated under the romantic comedy delusion that “doing everything together” was the ultimate metric of a successful union. If I went to a pottery class, I felt guilty for not inviting him. If he wanted to spend a Saturday morning working on his car, I felt like our bond was fraying because we weren’t eating brunch side-by-side.

We were confusing proximity with intimacy. It took about two years of friction—and a lot of irritability over nothing—to realize that our constant presence was actually stifling our connection. We weren’t two people choosing to be together; we were two people glued together, unable to move independently without causing a scene.

The “Autonomy Test” is a framework I started using to gauge whether we were in a healthy rhythm or a codependent trap. It’s not about how much time you spend together, but how much you are allowed to be yourself when you aren’t.

Identifying the “Absorption” Trap

The biggest sign that you’ve lost your autonomy is the “Absorption Trap.” This happens when your partner’s hobbies, opinions, and moods become your own because you’ve stopped cultivating your own reality.

Ask yourself this: If your partner suddenly disappeared for a weekend, would you have a plan for your time, or would you feel a sense of aimless dread? If you find yourself waiting for your partner to pick a restaurant, choose a movie, or decide how to spend a Friday night, you have handed over your decision-making autonomy.

I once had a friend who stopped reading fiction because her boyfriend thought novels were a “waste of time.” She didn’t consciously decide to quit reading; she just slowly let her interests evaporate to match his. That is the slow death of a relationship. When you shrink your world to fit into your partner’s, you eventually become resentful because you stop being the person they fell in love with in the first place.

The Test: Measuring Your Individual Perimeter

To keep a relationship healthy, you need a defined “perimeter.” This is the space where you operate as an individual, not a duo. You can test your current level of autonomy by looking at these three specific areas:

  • The Weekend Buffer: Do you have at least one recurring window of time—say, four hours on a Saturday or a full Sunday morning—where you pursue a hobby your partner finds boring?
  • The Opinion Audit: When you are asked for your take on a news story or a movie, do you answer based on your actual experience, or do you wait to hear what your partner says so you can mirror them?
  • The Friendship Tier: Do you have at least one friend you see regularly without your partner present, where the dynamic is entirely about your history and connection, not your couple identity?

If you struggle to answer these, you don’t need to break up, but you do need a reset. Start by reclaiming one hour a week for something you alone enjoy. Go to a coffee shop with a book, go for a hike, or sign up for a workshop. Do not check in, do not invite them, and do not apologize for it.

Why Proximity Is Not a Substitute for Intimacy

There is a common misconception that if you spend enough time together, you will eventually reach a state of perfect harmony. In reality, that is usually just boredom or burnout.

Intimacy requires two distinct, separate people coming together. If you are exactly the same—sharing the same schedule, the same friends, and the same narrow set of interests—you have nothing new to offer each other. A healthy relationship acts like a conversation. You go out into the world, gather new experiences, ideas, and perspectives, and you bring those back to your partner.

When I started taking my solo trips to the local climbing gym, I noticed our dinner conversations became much more interesting. I had something to tell him that wasn’t just “How was your day?” followed by a recounting of chores. I had a new challenge I’d faced, a person I’d met, or a frustration I’d overcome. We were finally dating each other again instead of just co-existing.

Setting Boundaries Without Starting a Fight

Reclaiming your autonomy can feel threatening to a partner who is used to having you around 24/7. They might perceive your need for space as a lack of interest or a sign of distance. This is where communication is crucial.

Don’t frame it as, “I need to get away from you.” Frame it as, “I want to be a more interesting and energized person for us.” Explain that when you have your own projects and social life, you feel more like yourself, which makes you a better partner.

If you are currently feeling suffocated or invisible in your relationship, it is time to shift your focus from “us” back to “me.” You cannot provide the best version of yourself to your partner if you are constantly depleting your own reservoir of interests and individuality.

Taking these steps is the first move toward a more sustainable connection. As you start to carve out this space, you will likely find that your partner respects you more—and perhaps, they might even be inspired to start their own autonomy project, too.
